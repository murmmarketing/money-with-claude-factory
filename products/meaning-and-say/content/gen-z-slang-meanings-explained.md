# Gen Z Slang, Explained: What 'Rizz', 'Delulu' and 'Mid' Actually Mean

If you've felt lost in a group chat lately, it's not you — slang has never moved faster. Words jump from one app to a million comment sections in days, meanings shift, and half of them are used ironically. This is a plain-English guide to the terms you're most likely to run into, what they really mean, and how to use them without sounding like you're trying too hard.

## The core vocabulary

- **Rizz** — charm or charisma, especially the skill of flirting well. "He got her number with one line — unspoken rizz." It's short for "charisma".
- **Delulu** — playfully delusional; holding hopes that are a little unrealistic, usually about romance or success. "I'm being delulu but I think we'll win." Say it **duh-LOO-loo**.
- **Mid** — mediocre or overrated; not as good as the hype. "The movie was mid." It's the single most efficient way to damn something with faint praise.
- **Based** — admirably true to yourself, unafraid of what others think. It's the opposite of **cringe**. "He owned his weird opinion — based."
- **Aura** — a running tally of coolness. Do something impressive and you gain "aura"; embarrass yourself and you lose it. "Caught the glass without looking — plus a thousand aura."

## The words that describe *behaviour*

A lot of modern slang exists to name social dynamics precisely:

- **Ghosting** — cutting off all contact with someone, no explanation.
- **Gaslighting** — manipulating someone into doubting their own memory or perception.
- **Simp** — someone who does far too much for a person they're attracted to.
- **Ick** — the sudden turn-off you feel over a tiny off-putting thing.
- **Ratio** — on social media, when a reply gets more likes than the post it's replying to, signalling the crowd disagrees.

## Why so much of it is ironic

Terms like **sigma**, **skibidi**, and **NPC** are used sincerely almost never. "Sigma" (a lone-wolf who succeeds on his own terms) started as self-help gym culture and is now mostly a joke. "Skibidi" is near-meaningless — a placeholder that means "good", "bad", or "weird" depending entirely on tone. Reading these literally is the fastest way to misuse them. The rule of thumb: the more absurd a word sounds, the more likely it's being used with a wink.

## How the words spread — and why meanings drift

Most of today's slang comes from three places: African American Vernacular English (AAVE), which gave us **bet**, **cap/no cap**, **finna**, and **lowkey**; drag and ballroom culture, which gave us **slay**, **tea**, and **periodt**; and gaming and streaming, which gave us **sus**, **NPC**, and **touch grass**. As a word travels out of its origin community, its meaning softens and broadens — which is exactly why a single dictionary line never quite captures it. A good example sentence does far more.
