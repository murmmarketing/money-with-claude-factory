# How to Avoid Customs Charges on Reps: De Minimis by Country (2026)

Customs is the single biggest wildcard in a reps haul. You can nail the W2C, get flawless QC, pick the cheapest line — and still get hit with a tax bill that turns a "cheap" haul into a bad deal. The good news: customs isn't random. It runs on published rules, and once you understand **de minimis**, you can plan around it.

This is a practical, country-by-country breakdown for 2026. It is not legal advice — declaration is your responsibility — but it will help you understand the math customs uses.

## What "de minimis" actually means

De minimis is the value threshold *below which* a country waives import tax and/or duty. Ship under it and, in principle, you pay nothing at the border. Ship over it and you owe VAT/GST and possibly duty on the declared value (often including shipping).

Two things trip people up:

- **De minimis for VAT and de minimis for duty are often different numbers.** A parcel can be VAT-liable but duty-free.
- **The declared value is what matters**, not what you actually paid. Under-declaration is a customs offence, so this guide assumes honest declaration and focuses on legitimate structuring (parcel size, timing, thresholds).

## Country-by-country (2026)

### 🇺🇸 United States
The big change: the US **ended the $800 de minimis exemption for shipments from China** in 2025. Parcels from Chinese origin no longer sail in tax-free under $800 the way they used to — expect duty/fees to apply and enforcement to be tighter. Treat US hauls as **taxable regardless of value** and budget for it. Duty rates vary wildly by product category (footwear and apparel are among the highest). US buyers now benefit most from *smaller, less frequent* parcels rather than relying on a threshold that no longer protects them.

### 🇬🇧 United Kingdom
- **VAT threshold:** goods valued **£135 or under** are VAT-charged at point of sale (the marketplace/carrier collects 20%); above £135, VAT + potential duty are collected at import.
- **Duty:** generally kicks in above **£135** and depends on category.
- **Practical read:** a haul declared under £135 pays 20% VAT but avoids the messier duty + handling-fee layer. Over £135, add a courier "handling fee" (£8–£12) on top.

### 🇪🇺 European Union
The sharpest wedge, and the reason EU buyers do the most landed-cost math:
- **VAT de minimis: €0.** There is no VAT-free threshold — VAT applies to essentially every parcel via the IOSS system.
- **Duty de minimis: €150.** Below €150 declared value, you pay VAT but **no customs duty**. Above €150, duty applies on top of VAT.
- **VAT rate varies by country:** 19% (Germany), 20% (France), 21% (Netherlands/Spain), 22% (Italy), up to 27% (Hungary).
- **Practical read:** keep each parcel **under €150** to stay duty-free, and always price VAT in — it's unavoidable. A €300 haul split into two €150 parcels can dodge duty entirely while paying the same VAT.

### 🇨🇦 Canada
- **De minimis:** roughly **CAD $20** for tax purposes on most commercial shipments (a courier-specific CAD $40 tax / $150 duty relief exists under CUSMA for US/Mexico origin, which does **not** apply to China-origin reps).
- **Practical read:** almost every reps parcel is taxable. Expect GST/HST (5–15% depending on province) plus possible duty and a brokerage fee. Canada is one of the least de-minimis-friendly destinations.

### 🇦🇺 Australia
- **De minimis:** **AUD $1,000**. Goods at or under AUD $1,000 are generally **GST-free at the border** for low-value imports (GST is instead collected at sale by registered suppliers, which agents often don't apply).
- **Practical read:** Australia is the most forgiving major destination. Most single hauls fall under AUD $1,000 and clear without a border tax bill. Above $1,000 you owe 10% GST + duty + processing charges.

## Split-shipment: the legitimate tactic

For threshold countries (UK £135, EU €150 duty, AU $1,000), splitting one large haul into multiple parcels each under the threshold can legitimately reduce tax — *if* the extra shipping cost is less than the tax you'd otherwise pay.

The trade-off math:
- Splitting adds a second parcel's **base shipping cost** (and you lose consolidation efficiency).
- It can save the **duty** on the whole declared value.

Worked EU example (haul declared €280, destination Germany):
- **One parcel:** VAT 19% on €280 = €53.20, plus duty (~12% apparel) €33.60 → **~€86.80 tax**
- **Two parcels of €140:** VAT 19% on €280 total = €53.20, **duty €0** (both under €150) → **~€53.20 tax**, minus ~€30 extra shipping = still **~€3+ ahead**, and far ahead on larger hauls.

The bigger the haul, the more splitting wins. Below the threshold already? Don't split — you'd just pay double base shipping for nothing.

## Handling fees: the hidden line item

Even when tax is small, carriers add a **customs handling / brokerage fee** for clearing your parcel (£8–£12 UK, €8–€20 EU, CAD $10–$20). Always add this to your landed cost — it's the fee people forget until the courier holds the parcel hostage at the door.
