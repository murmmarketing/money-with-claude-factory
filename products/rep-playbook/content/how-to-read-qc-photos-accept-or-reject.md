# How to Read QC Photos: Accept or Reject Your Rep Before It Ships

QC — quality control — is the single moment where you decide whether your rep is a keeper or a mistake. Your agent photographs the item at their warehouse and waits for you to **approve** or **refuse** before shipping. Once it ships, it's yours. So this ten-minute check is the most valuable skill a rep buyer can learn. Here's how to actually read the photos instead of glancing and clicking "ship."

## What QC photos are (and aren't)

QC photos are quick warehouse snaps — decent lighting, not studio quality. Don't reject for a bad angle or a shadow; that's the photographer, not the product. You're judging the **item**, not the photography. If a detail is genuinely unclear, most agents let you **request additional photos** (an "extra QC"). Use it — it's usually free or cheap and far better than guessing.

## Set up your comparison first

Open **official retail photos** of the exact item — right colorway, right model — in another tab. You can't judge accuracy from memory. Rep quality is always relative to the real thing, so put them side by side before you form an opinion.

## The universal QC checklist

Work top to bottom, every time:

1. **Logos and branding.** Correct font, spacing, size and placement. Wrong kerning, a crooked logo, or a misspelled tag is an instant red flag.
2. **Colorway.** Compare against retail under similar lighting. Warehouse light shifts tones, but an obviously wrong shade (too blue, too washed out) is real.
3. **Stitching.** Straight, even, no loose threads, no skipped rows. Sloppy stitching is the most common giveaway of a bad batch.
4. **Materials and texture.** Leather grain, knit texture, hardware finish. Plasticky where it should be metal, or shiny where it should be matte, means a lower batch.
5. **Shape and proportion.** Does the silhouette match retail? Toe box on shoes, collar on a jacket, hardware size on a bag.
6. **Symmetry.** Left vs. right shoe, left vs. right panel. Alignment should mirror.
7. **The details buyers forget.** Insoles and heel tabs on shoes, zipper pulls and lining on bags, wash tags and interior labels on clothing.

## Category-specific red flags

- **Sneakers:** uneven midsole paint, wrong toe-box shape, misaligned stitching on the swoosh/stripe, sloppy heel logo, off insole print.
- **Hoodies / tees:** wrong logo placement or size, thin/uneven print, off fabric weight, crooked embroidery, wrong tag font.
- **Bags:** uneven hardware, wrong stamp/emboss depth, misaligned pattern at seams, cheap-feeling lining, loose piping.
- **Watches:** misaligned date window, wrong dial text spacing, weak lume, rough bezel action.

## The accept / reject decision

Ask one question: **would this bother me every time I wear it?**

- **Approve** if the flaws are minor, non-visible, or things you genuinely won't notice in use.
- **Refuse (RL / return-label)** if there's a visible, permanent defect: wrong colorway, glaring logo error, torn material, deep stains, or clearly the wrong item/size.

Every agent handles rejection slightly differently — refund vs. re-order, who eats the return shipping — so know your agent's QC policy **before** you order, not after.

## Common beginner QC mistakes

- **Approving instantly** to move things along. This is how bad items ship.
- **Judging the photo, not the product.** Blurry ≠ defective. Ask for more shots.
- **No retail comparison**, so you can't tell "batch difference" from "defect."
- **Ignoring size in QC.** If the photo shows a size tag that doesn't match your order, catch it now.
- **Over-rejecting.** No rep is a 1:1 twin of retail. Chasing perfection wastes reships and money.
